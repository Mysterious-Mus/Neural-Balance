\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{What regularization is}
In supervised learning, regularization is any mechanism that improves generalization beyond minimizing training loss. In neural networks, this can take the form of explicit penalties on parameters, stochastic perturbations during training, constraints induced by optimization procedures, or transformations applied to parameters while preserving the represented function.

\subsection{Common regularization families}
We group regularization methods into several broad families:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Explicit penalties} such as $L_2$ weight decay and sparsity-inducing $L_1$ penalties.
  \item \textbf{Stochastic regularization} such as dropout and noise injection.
  \item \textbf{Data and training procedure} choices such as data augmentation and early stopping.
  \item \textbf{Normalization and re-parameterization} methods that change optimization geometry (often interacting with regularization).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Synaptic neural balance in this context}
Synaptic neural balance is a re-scaling transformation defined at the level of individual neurons (or layers) that aims to equalize the ``cost'' of incoming and outgoing weights under a chosen regularizer, without changing the network function under conditions described in the reference paper \citep{alexos2025synapticbalance}. This makes it natural to study neural balancing alongside classical regularizers, either as a preprocessing step or interleaved with training.
