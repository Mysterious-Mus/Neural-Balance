Regularization is central to training neural networks that generalize well, especially in data-scarce or noisy regimes. This report surveys and empirically compares common regularization approaches---including weight decay, dropout, early stopping, data augmentation, and normalization-based techniques---with a focus on how they affect optimization dynamics, calibration, and generalization.

A main emphasis is \emph{synaptic neural balance} (also referred to as \emph{neural balancing}), a method that re-scales a network's weights to equalize per-neuron incoming and outgoing weight ``cost'' under a chosen regularizer, without changing the function represented by the network under broad conditions. Building on the reference material in \texttt{reference/}, we position neural balancing as a complementary tool to classical regularizers and evaluate when it helps, how sensitive it is to design choices (e.g., $L_1$ vs.\ $L_2$ cost), and how it interacts with standard training pipelines.
